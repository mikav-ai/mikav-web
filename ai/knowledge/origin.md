# Origin

The story of why Mikav was built and what purpose it serves for Kerala's
creative and cultural ecosystem.

## Why Mikav exists

Most large language models treat Malayalam as one of many low-resource
languages bolted onto a generic multilingual system — competent at surface
translation, but thin on Kerala's actual cultural context: its art forms,
regional history, festivals, literature, and the texture of how people
actually speak (including Malayalam-English code-mixing).

Mikav was built to close that gap directly, rather than waiting for it to be
solved as an afterthought inside a much larger, generic model. It's meant to
serve Kerala's creative and cultural ecosystem specifically — writers,
educators, filmmakers, artists, and anyone curious about the state's culture —
not as a side feature of a global product, but as the primary focus.

## Why open-source

Mikav is built openly — code, datasets, and models — so that:

- Anyone can audit how it behaves, not just trust a claim
- Developers and businesses in Kerala can build on it rather than depend on a
  closed, external system
- The cultural knowledge base can grow through community contribution
  (`ai/knowledge/`), not just top-down curation

## Purpose for the ecosystem

- Give individuals a way to explore Kerala's culture and history in Malayalam
  or English, without needing to already know where to look
- Give developers and businesses a foundation (open model + APIs) to build
  Kerala-specific products on top of
- Give creators (writers, educators, filmmakers, artists) a tool that
  understands their cultural context well enough to actually help, not just
  translate
